# **Pabau content style guide**

Welcome\! This guide will help you write content that sounds like Pabau — clear, warm, and useful to the people we serve. Whether you're working on a blog, a knowledge base article, social media copy, or website content, the principles here apply.

Pabau is a global company, and our key growth driver is US expansion — so we lean toward American English as our default (e.g., *customize*, not *customise*). The key terminology differences are flagged in the [Words we use](https://claude.ai/chat/be39c54d-f695-43f7-b134-a4259bce39e5#words-we-use) section below.

This is a living document. If you spot something missing or want to suggest a change, let us know.

---

## **Our tone of voice**

Our writing should feel like talking to a trusted friend. Here's what that means in practice:

**Human, not corporate.** Our readers are smart, but they're not corporate. They're practitioners and entrepreneurs running their own thing — not executives in a boardroom. Write to them like you'd talk to a sharp friend who happens to run a clinic. 

If it sounds stiff when you read it back, it's too formal.

**Clear and simple.** Demystify wherever you can. Swap long words for shorter ones. Break up long sentences. Use lists, tables, before-and-after scenarios, and images to make complex ideas easier to grasp. This matters most for legislation and compliance, which are often unnecessarily complicated.

**Quick to the point.** Our customers are busy. Don't bury the answer in long paragraphs — they'll skim and tune out. If you can say it in two lines instead of four, do.

**Pitched at the right level.** Our readers are sharp, but they don't necessarily nerd out on software the way SaaS people do. They're running clinics, treating patients, building businesses — many for the first time. Skip the jargon, acronyms, and marketing-speak — if you'd translate a phrase for a friend, translate it before it hits the page.

**Educational, not salesy.** We're here to help everyone do a little bit better every day. That might be a big shift (paper to digital records) or a small one (using more of the features they already have access to).

**Add some personality.** Your writing doesn't have to be dull just because the topic is serious. A bit of personality keeps readers with you — just make sure it feels natural, not forced.

## **Writing principles**

### **Write how you speak**

Use contractions (*we've*, *it's*, *you'll*) to make your writing flow. They sound natural and friendly.

Vary your sentence length. Lots of long sentences make the reader work too hard. Mix it up. Even a one-word sentence can land hard.

### **Always introduce Pabau**

If someone arrives at a blog from Google, they've probably never heard of us. Don't write just *"Pabau"* — write *"Practice management software like Pabau...."*

### **Be specific about benefits**

Vague benefit statements lose readers. Paint a picture instead.

Don't write: *"Pabau boosts operational efficiency."* What does that even mean? Drop it down a level. For our customers, *operational efficiency* might mean:

* Your team doesn't have to call clients to confirm appointments  
* You don't have to manually send pre- and aftercare instructions  
* You can finish work on time instead of replying to client DMs at 9pm

A handy framework (borrowed from SaaS marketer Jess Cook) is *"so you can…"*:

* *Swap paper for digital client records so you can work from anywhere.*  
* *Use an online booking tool so you can save your team hours each week.*

**Swap this:**

Our solution supercharges your operations process.

**For this:**

Our online booking platform helps med spa owners reduce no-shows through customizable deposits. Fewer unreliable patients booking \= fewer wasted appointment slots.

### **Spell out the benefit**

Don't assume readers will work out the benefit of a feature for themselves. They won't. They'll skim, and if the value isn't right there in the words, they'll keep skimming.

Take the client portal. Vague claims like *"boosts operational efficiency"* or *"streamlines your workflow"* don't paint a picture — readers can't see themselves in them. Spell out what it actually does in someone's day. What can they stop doing manually? What headache disappears once it's set up?

Don't bury the benefit, don't imply it, don't leave it for the reader to join the dots.

❌ *"The client portal boosts operational efficiency."* ✅ *"With the client portal, clients can rebook, reschedule, or cancel themselves — so your front desk isn't fielding 30 calls a day about appointment changes."*

### **Be human**

Our customers care about outcomes, not technology. They want:

* More time for the parts of the job they love  
* A clear head at the end of the day  
* To finish on time, not message clients after hours  
* Happy clients and an easier life

Lead with the emotional hook — what the technology unlocks for someone's day. Not the technology itself.

### **Push the value, not the product**

Our customers might love their Apple Watches and have all the smart home gadgets — but professionally, their attention is on running their clinic, treating patients, and getting home on time, not on evaluating SaaS tools or learning marketing jargon.

So lead with what the technology unlocks — the time saved, the headache avoided, the smoother day — not the technology itself.

### **Don't overload the benefits**

Pabau does a lot. But if you list everything in one go, readers will tune out. Pick three or four benefits that are most relevant to the topic you're writing about — that's the sweet spot.

For example, if you're writing about the client portal, you don't need to mention every single thing the portal does. Focus on the specific pain point you're addressing.

**Swap this:**

Eliminate risk by improving discovery, detection, and defense while reducing cost, minimizing compliance risk, fraud, business abuse, and data losses.

**For this:**

Improve your defense while reducing cost.

### **Explain what you mean**

Define acronyms the first time you use them. Don't assume the reader knows.

✅ *Protected health information, known as PHI, can reveal a patient's identity.* ❌ *You shouldn't share PHI because it can reveal a patient's identity.*

### **Be mindful of internal terminology**

Our products have internal names that won't mean anything to someone who isn't already a customer. *Pabau GO* is just two words to a first-time reader — it doesn't tell them anything about what the product actually does.

When you use a product name externally, always qualify it with a quick description:

* *Pabau GO, our iOS app*  
* *Pabau Scribe, our AI scribe*  
* *Pabau Pay, our card terminals*

Once you've introduced the product, you can use the name freely in the rest of the piece.

**One important exception: don't use *Pabau Connect* in external content.** It's an internal name for our online booking, and customers won't recognize it.

Just say *"online booking"* or *"our online booking portal."*

### **Don't undermine our own products**

When you're describing one part of Pabau, be careful not to make another part sound weak by comparison.

This trips writers up most often with the **Plus add-ons.** Calling them *"extended"* or *"advanced"* marketing tools implicitly suggests the core marketing is limited — when every Pabau subscription actually comes with a full marketing suite, patient care, and reporting functionality. Use *"additional"* or *"specialist"* instead.

The same trap shows up in **competitor comparisons.** If you're contrasting Pabau with a tool that does one specific thing well, don't throw one of Pabau's own capabilities under the bus to make the comparison land.

**Rule of thumb:** frame distinctions as *more on top*, not *better than what you already have*. For example:

* ❌ *"Marketing Plus gives you the proper automation tools that the basic Pabau marketing lacks."*  
* ✅ *"Marketing Plus adds advanced automation on top of Pabau's built-in marketing suite."*

**One more thing on audience.** When you're writing for prospects — people who aren't Pabau customers yet — lead on the breadth of the core subscription. 

*"Pabau has a full marketing suite built in"* is already a strong message, because plenty of competing tools don't have these capabilities at all. Save Plus mentions for content aimed at existing customers or content where a Plus product is specifically the subject. Upselling someone who hasn't bought the base product yet just muddies the message.

### **Use formatting to draw the eye**

Use **bold** for important points you want readers to land on. Use CAPS for emphasis when you're SUPER enthusiastic. Both work best when used sparingly — less is more.

Use plenty of white space, lists, tables, and before-and-after scenarios. If a visual or table will make the content clearer, add it — even if it's not in the brief. Always write for the reader's experience.

For key takeaways in blog posts, use blue panels in Elementor (see the blog creation documentation for how-to).

Use emojis sparingly in blogs — they're fine for highlighting a header or breaking up bullets, but not everywhere. We have more flexibility on social.

### **Customize your CTAs**

Make CTAs specific to what you've just covered, not generic.

**Generic:**

Book a demo today.

**Customized:**

If you'd like to see exactly how Pabau's email communications can help your med spa get more bookings, schedule a demo today.

Like to see \[this feature\] in action? Schedule a 60-minute meeting with our team.

### **Don't say "in conclusion"**

It sounds like a school essay. Try one of these instead:

* *To sum up…*  
* *The key takeaway is…*  
* *So, there you have it.*  
* *It's probably clear by now…*

### **Meet the search intent**

Google ranks content that answers the search query quickly. Don't make readers dig.

Use headers, jump links, and formatting to surface the answer fast. A handy structure: give the short answer first (*"Yes, online booking tools do boost revenue."*), then dive into the detail.

This applies to Knowledge Base content too — customers often arrive directly from a search and want an answer right away. Don't make them wade through lots of text.

## **Words we use**

### **American English by default**

We've moved from a UK-focused brand to a global one, so we lean toward American English. Most of our blog briefs are built from US keyword research.

| Use this (US) | Not this (UK) |
| ----- | ----- |
| Customize | Customise |
| Practice (verb and noun) | Practise (verb), practice (noun) |
| Enroll | Enrol |
| Catalog | Catalogue |
| Specialty | Speciality |
| Defense | Defence |

When you cite statistics, lean toward US sources first. UK sources are fine if that's what you can find.

### **Industry terminology**

We work globally, so reference both terms when there's a meaningful split — for example, *"med spas and medi aesthetics clinics"* or *"private practices and GPs."*

| US term | UK term |
| ----- | ----- |
| Med spa | Medi aesthetics clinic |
| Physician | Doctor |
| Physical therapy | Physiotherapy |
| Esthetician | Beauty therapist / beautician |
| Private practice, primary care, family physician | GP |
| Intake form | Medical questionnaire |
| Injection plotting | Image mark-up |
| Charting | Treatment notes |
| Consent form | Release form / intake form |
| Medical director | Clinic director / Lead doctor |
| EMR | EHR |
| Client record | Patient record |
| GMC number | NPA number |
| Payment / Invoicing | Billing |
| Stock | Inventory |
| Pre- and aftercare | Pre- and post-care |
| Schedule / staff schedule | Rota |
| Reception / receptionist | Front of house |
| Telehealth | Telemedicine, video consultations |
| Insurance billing | Private billing |

**Clinical and billing codes.** ICD codes (International Classification of Diseases) are used globally for diagnostic coding — there's no US/UK split there.

Procedure codes do differ: US clinics use **CPT codes** (Current Procedural Terminology), UK clinics use **OPCS codes** (Office of Population Censuses and Surveys Classification of Surgical Operations and Procedures).

If you're writing about coding or billing workflows, reference both where it applies.

### **Words to swap**

Long, formal words slow readers down. Try these swaps:

| Swap this | For this |
| ----- | ----- |
| Utilize | Use |
| Facilitate | Help |
| Commence | Start |
| Due to the fact that | Because |
| At this point in time | Now |
| Moreover | Plus / Also / Additionally |
| In the event of | If |
| In as short a time as possible | Quickly |

## **Formatting and mechanics**

### **Numbers**

Spell out numbers under 10\. Use numerals for 10 and above.

✅ *Respect is one of our values. There are 12 values.* ❌ *Respect is 1 of our values. There are twelve values.*

### **Dates**

Use the format *month day, year*.

✅ *We're hosting an event on September 15, 2026\.* ❌ *We're hosting an event on the 15th of September 2026\.*

### **Punctuation**

* Use em dashes ( — ) with a space on either side instead of semicolons. However, just be mindful they are synonymous with ai content now, so be sparing with them.   
* Avoid semicolons. They feel formal.  
* Avoid ampersands — write *salons and spas*, not *salons & spas*.

### **Headers**

Use sentence case, not title case.

✅ *Why choose Pabau as your practice management software* ❌ *Why Choose Pabau As Your Practice Management Software*

### **Abbreviations**

Write things out in full rather than abbreviating (e.g., *information*, not *info*).

For locations and times, no periods:

* *UK*, *US* (not *U.K.*, *U.S.*)  
* *AM*, *PM* (not *A.M.*, *P.M.*)

### **Acronyms**

Define an acronym the first time it appears, then use the acronym freely.

✅ *Protected health information, known as PHI, can reveal a patient's identity. To stay compliant, never share PHI…*

### **Citing sources**

If a source is well known (Forbes, Harper's Bazaar, the American Med Spa Association), name it. If it isn't, we don’t need to name it.

* *A recent study found…*  
* *Experts agree…*  
* *A study published in a top medical journal…*

This stops sentences getting bogged down in unfamiliar detail.

## **Glossary**

### **Treatments and procedures**

**11s** — Colloquial term for the two vertical lines between the eyebrows (so called because they resemble the number 11). Clinical name: *glabellar lines* or *frown lines*. Often the target of botulinum toxin injections.

**Blepharoplasty** — Eyelid surgery. Often shortened to *bleph* in conversation (*upper bleph*, *lower bleph*). It's surgical, so worth flagging the surgical/non-surgical distinction in content where relevant.

**Botox** — Brand name (Allergan) for a botulinum toxin Type A injectable. Measured in *units*. Other brands in this category include *Dysport*, *Xeomin*, *Bocouture*, and *Azzalure*. Commonly used generically in conversation, but in clinical content the generic term is *botulinum toxin* or *anti-wrinkle injections*. **UK note:** Botox is a prescription-only medicine — see the *POMs* entry under *Regulation and compliance* for advertising rules.

**Dermal fillers** — Injectable hyaluronic-acid (HA) fillers used to add volume or reshape features. Measured in *ml*. *Juvederm* is the most widely used brand, with others including *Restylane*, *Belotero*, and *Teosyal*. Specific treatment variants include *lip filler*, *cheek filler*, and *non-surgical rhinoplasty*.

**HIFU** — High-Intensity Focused Ultrasound. A non-invasive skin-tightening treatment.

**Hydrafacial** — A popular treatment in med spas. Styled as *Hydrafacial* (not *hydra-facial* or *HydraFacial*).

**IPL** — Intense Pulsed Light. Used for hair removal and skin rejuvenation.

**IV therapy** — Intravenous drip treatments. In the UK these are CQC in-scope, meaning a clinic must be CQC-registered to offer them.

**LED light therapy** — Light therapy using red, blue, or infrared LEDs, commonly used for skin concerns like acne, redness, and collagen stimulation. Distinct from UV light therapy, which is dermatological (for conditions like psoriasis) rather than aesthetic.

**Microneedling** — Collagen-induction therapy using fine needles to stimulate skin renewal.

**Non-surgical rhinoplasty** — Nose reshaping using dermal fillers rather than surgery. Sometimes marketed as *liquid rhinoplasty*.

**PRP** — Platelet-Rich Plasma. A treatment using components separated from a patient's own blood. The *vampire facial* is a marketing name for PRP combined with microneedling.

**Profhilo** — Brand name for an injectable skin booster (not a filler). Capitalize as written.

**Thread lift** — Non-surgical face lift using dissolvable threads.

### **Regulation and compliance**

**Care Quality Commission (CQC)** — The regulator for health and social care in England. Some treatments (like IV drips) are "in scope," meaning a clinic must be CQC-registered to administer them.

**HIPAA compliance** — The US health data privacy law. Note the spelling: HIPAA, not HIPPA.

**HIS** — In Scotland, independent healthcare services, including aesthetic clinics run by healthcare professionals, are regulated by Healthcare Improvement Scotland (HIS). Clinics providing regulated services must be registered with HIS and meet its standards covering patient safety, clinical governance, and quality of care. 

**POMs (Prescription-Only Medicines)** — UK term for medicines that require a prescription. In UK content, POMs cannot be advertised to consumers by name — they must be referred to generically (for example, *anti-wrinkle injections* instead of *Botox*). Common POMs in aesthetics include botulinum toxin (all brands), weight-loss injectables (Wegovy, Mounjaro, Ozempic), and prescription skincare like tretinoin.

### **Codes and clinical references**

**ICD codes** — International Classification of Diseases. Diagnostic coding standard used globally — no US/UK split.

**CPT codes** — Current Procedural Terminology. Procedure coding used in the US.

**OPCS codes** — Office of Population Censuses and Surveys Classification of Surgical Operations and Procedures. Procedure coding used in the UK.

### **Roles and business models**

**A quick note on practitioner roles:** aesthetics has a complex regulatory landscape — who can do what depends heavily on jurisdiction.

In the **US**, most rules vary state by state, including supervision requirements, prescribing authority, and medical director rules. In the **UK**, the system is more centralized but has specific nuance, particularly around prescribing rights.

Don't make absolute claims in content without checking the relevant rules. When in doubt, qualify with *"depending on state"* (US) or *"depending on qualification"* (UK).

**Aesthetic nurse / Cosmetic nurse** — General term for a registered nurse specializing in aesthetic treatments. Independence, prescribing rights, and scope vary by jurisdiction (and by their individual qualification — see *Nurse Prescriber* and *Nurse Practitioner*).

**Doctor / Physician** — A qualified medical practitioner with prescribing rights. Can perform and prescribe the full range of aesthetic treatments. In aesthetics, often an *aesthetic doctor*, *dermatologist*, or *cosmetic/plastic surgeon* by specialty.

**Esthetician** — A skin care specialist who performs treatments like facials and lasers. In the UK, called a *beauty therapist* or *beautician*. Non-medical — can't administer injectables or prescription treatments.

**Medical Director** — A licensed physician overseeing the medical/clinical aspects of a clinic. In the US, many states require med spas to have a medical director on file, but the specific requirements (physical presence, supervision ratios, allowed delegation) vary by state.

**Nurse Practitioner (NP)** — *US.* Advanced practice nurse qualified to assess, diagnose, and (in most states) prescribe. Independent practice authority varies by state — some allow fully independent practice, others require physician collaboration or supervision. NPs frequently lead aesthetic practices.

**Nurse Prescriber** — *UK.* A registered nurse who has completed an independent prescribing qualification, allowing them to prescribe POMs (like botulinum toxin) within their competence. Without this qualification, an RN can't prescribe — they'd be working from another prescriber's instructions.

**Physician Assistant (PA)** — *US.* Healthcare provider qualified to assess, diagnose, and treat under physician supervision. Prescribing authority and scope vary by state.

**Prescriber** — Umbrella term for anyone qualified to prescribe medicines. In aesthetics that typically means doctors, dentists, nurse prescribers (UK) or nurse practitioners (US), or pharmacist prescribers (UK). *"Working under a prescriber"* describes a practitioner who isn't qualified to prescribe themselves administering treatments on another prescriber's authority.

**Registered Nurse (RN)** — In both US and UK, an RN cannot prescribe medicines unless they hold an additional prescribing qualification (see *Nurse Practitioner* or *Nurse Prescriber*). They can typically administer treatments under a prescriber's instructions, but the exact scope varies by jurisdiction.

**MSO (Management Service Organization)** — A US arrangement where a med spa outsources the business side of operations while the medical team focuses on patient care. (See our blog for more.)
