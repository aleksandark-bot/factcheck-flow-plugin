# **About Pabau: Content team guide**

A working reference for the content team. It covers what Pabau is and who buys it, where we sit in a crowded market, what's in the product family and how it's priced, and how customers experience us from signup onwards.

The goal is to give you the commercial and competitive context behind what you're writing about, so when you're choosing what to emphasize in a piece, you know where it sits in the bigger picture.

This isn't a sales deck. It's a working reference — read it once, then dip back in when you need a refresh.

---

## **What Pabau is**

Pabau is an all-in-one practice management system.

Most clinics start out juggling separate tools — paper records for clinical notes, a booking system for appointments, a spreadsheet for inventory, a card terminal that doesn't talk to anything else.

Pabau replaces that patchwork by bringing clinical care, business operations, and patient engagement together in one system.

EMR sits alongside scheduling, payments, marketing, reporting, inventory, and consent — so a patient record, an appointment, an invoice, and a treatment note all live in the same place.

No more importing and exporting data between tools that weren't designed to talk to each other.

A lot of tools claim they can do everything. Not all of them deliver.

Pabau is built to handle the actual complexity of medical workflows — managing a patient journey from first enquiry through to follow-up, with the clinical detail and compliance that goes with it — not just the booking-and-payments layer that lighter tools cover.

**For your writing:** the headline framing is *all-in-one practice management system* — that's our standard external phrase, and the patchwork (paper records \+ a separate booking tool, and so on) is exactly the situation it replaces.

Go deeper with the breadth angle — *clinical care, business operations, and patient engagement* all in one place — when you need to differentiate from lighter all-in-ones, which handle bookings and payments fine but fall over when patient journeys get involved.

## **Who our customers are**

Pabau's core vertical is medical aesthetics — that's what the product was purpose-built for, and where it's the strongest fit.

More recently, we've added clinical features like prescribing to broaden our fit into private practices and other medical specialties too.

Day to day, customers span a range of areas:

* Medical aesthetics  
* Wellness and preventive care  
* Women's and men's health  
* Musculoskeletal and performance  
* Mental health and lifestyle  
* Private practice and primary care  
* And clinical specialties on top — cosmetic surgery, dermatology, IV therapy, weight loss, and others

Pabau is used across the full size spectrum, from solo practitioners through to enterprise groups with multiple locations.

Inside those businesses, the people using Pabau are practice owners, practice managers, treatment providers, front-of-house staff, and marketers — usually a mix, depending on the size of the team.

They're not necessarily the type to nerd out on software.

They didn't get into aesthetics or medicine because they love systems — they use Pabau because running a business demands it, and the alternative (paper records, disconnected tools, endless spreadsheets) is worse.

### **What they care about**

The wins our customers care about aren't really about software. They want:

* **More time treating patients**, less time on admin  
* **A smoother day** — not chasing missing info or fielding *"have I been booked in?"* calls  
* **Happy clients** who come back and refer their friends  
* **Finishing on time** — not replying to client messages at 9pm  
* **Confidence in the system** — patient data safe, compliance handled, nothing falling through the cracks  
* **Room to scale without complexity** — growth without the tech becoming a bottleneck

### **What worries them**

Knowing the fears matters too, because empathetic content speaks to these directly:

* **Picking the wrong system** and being stuck with something that doesn't fit how they actually run their business  
* **A nightmare setup** — losing weeks to migration, training, or workarounds  
* **Compliance risk** — patient data, GDPR, HIPAA, CQC, and the cost of getting any of it wrong  
* **Tech failures damaging the client experience** — a clinic running on technology lives or dies by it working  
* **Hidden costs** — promises that turn out to need add-ons or pricier tiers to actually deliver

**For your writing:** the lens for everything you write is that our customers want the *outcomes* Pabau delivers, not the software itself.

Speak to their wins and fears, not features. *"Get home on time"* lands harder than *"automated workflows."*

## **Where we sit in the market**

The practice management software market is crowded, and new tools launch all the time.

Most aren't direct equivalents of Pabau — they come from different starting points, serve different verticals, and price themselves at different points.

And often, the most relevant competitor isn't another software product at all — it's the patchwork of free tools, spreadsheets, and paper a clinic is already using.

It's worth understanding the whole landscape so you know who we're really up against in any given piece of content.

### **The status quo**

The competitor isn't always another software platform.

A practitioner coming to Pabau might be running on a near-full patchwork of free or generic tools, or might have a few solid pieces in place — say a dedicated EMR for clinical records — but everything else stitched together manually.

Every clinic's setup is different. Here's a flavour of what practitioners might be doing before they come to Pabau:

* **Patient records** — anything from a dedicated EMR through to Excel, Google Sheets, or paper folders.  
* **Consent forms** — PDFs from template sites, printed, signed, scanned, and manually uploaded to a client file; or a separate digital forms tool (like JotForm) that handles signing but doesn't connect to client records.  
* **Treatment notes** — handwritten on paper, typed into Word, or simply not recorded at all.  
* **Appointments** — a generic tool like Calendly that wasn't built for clinical workflows, or bookings flying in across phone calls, emails, and Instagram DMs all at once.  
* **Before-and-after photos** — the practitioner's personal phone, with everything sitting on the camera roll. Plenty of compliance risk there.  
* **Client communication** — WhatsApp, personal text messages, or Instagram DMs, with no record kept against the client file.  
* **Email marketing** — Mailchimp or similar, with no connection to client records or treatment history.  
* **Reminders** — the front-of-house team phoning every booking the morning of, or someone hand-typing reminder texts one at a time.  
* **Pre- and aftercare** — emails typed out fresh after each appointment, or printed handouts.  
* **Inventory** — a spreadsheet, or no system at all.  
* **Deposits and payments** — a standalone card terminal that doesn't talk to anything else, plus deposits chased over email and taken via manual bank transfers.

Newer clinics often have the full patchwork — no central system at all.

More established clinics usually have a tool or two in place, but the workarounds have built up around the edges over time.

Either way, the same problem applies: tools that don't talk to each other, the owner holding the whole picture in their head, and data copied between systems by hand.

As Dr Jordan Faulkner of Allo Aesthetics put it:

*"Your instinct at the start of creating a business is to try and pull back on costs and things like IT software, but actually that's quite shortsighted. By investing in something like that, it frees up your time to focus on those more important things."*

— **Dr Jordan Faulkner**, Founder and Lead Injector, Allo Aesthetics ([case study](https://pabau.com/case-studies/allo-aesthetics/))

**For your writing:** for many prospects, this is the real comparison — not Pabau vs another platform, but Pabau vs the patchwork they're currently juggling.

Lean into the consolidation story (*"one system instead of seven"*) and the cost of the current setup, both in lost time and in compliance risk. Patient data on personal phones is a particularly vivid example.

For newer clinics, the Allo Aesthetics quote above is the *"invest properly from day one"* angle in customer-voice form — getting the foundation right saves years of patchwork and the eventual painful switch.

### **Different tools, different starting points**

Pabau plays in healthcare and medical aesthetics, so pure salon and beauty platforms aren't really direct competitors.

The names below are either in our space already, or have moved into it from adjacent markets:

* **Phorest** — built originally for salons, with aesthetics functionality added later through an integration. That integration is why you'll see aesthetic clinics using it.  
* **Zenoti** — one of the most direct competitors in our space, especially in med spas and at the enterprise or multi-location end. Strong on running operations at scale.  
* **Jane** — focused on allied health (physio, mental health). Strong for that vertical, but marketing isn't as central to the platform as it is in Pabau.  
* **Cliniko** — popular with allied health practices (physio, chiro, and similar). Frequently compared with Jane in those verticals.  
* **Fresha** — historically a free tool, now with a monthly subscription. Sits at the cheaper end of the market and operates a marketplace-style model.

Each of these has grown out of a specific vertical or operating model, which shapes what they're good at — and where they fall short.

**For your writing:** be specific when you reference competitors. *"Pabau vs Jane"* lands very differently from *"Pabau vs every other practice management tool out there"* — readers want the comparison that applies to them.

Lean on each tool's strengths and starting point rather than vague swipes.

### **Freemium and marketplace tools**

Some tools market themselves as free — no monthly subscription.

The trade-off is that they charge commission on every new customer booked through them. The headline number is zero, but the running cost can stack up fast for clinics doing real volume.

A related pattern is the marketplace model: tools like Fresha route bookings through an online marketplace where many salons and clinics are listed side-by-side.

There are upsides (visibility, frictionless booking), but real trade-offs too:

* A customer searching for one specific clinic might end up browsing and booking a competitor instead.  
* Listings are templated, so there's less control over branding. Each clinic sits inside the marketplace's brand, rather than standing on its own.

**For your writing:** if you're comparing Pabau to a free or marketplace tool, the angle isn't *"we cost more, they cost nothing."* It's *"free isn't free once you run the numbers"* — and Pabau's flat subscription means customers can grow their bookings without paying more for the privilege.

The marketplace risk is also worth surfacing: customers usually want full control over their brand and client relationships, not to be one listing among many.

### **Cheaper subscription tools**

Other products sit at a lower price point than Pabau. We're not the cheap option, and we don't try to be.

Pabau is a more capable platform, and most customers who choose us are looking for software that can grow with their business rather than something they'll outgrow in 18 months.

**For your writing:** if you're going head-to-head with a cheaper tool, don't apologize for the price.

Lead on capability and growth — what customers will be able to do once they have Pabau in place, not the monthly cost.

### **Our footprint in the market**

Customers come to Pabau through the usual SaaS routes — inbound marketing, organic search, referrals, sales demos.

A couple of things about our footprint are worth knowing about too:

**Academy partnerships.** Well-known UK aesthetics academies — run by recognized brands and doctors — recommend Pabau as part of their training for newly qualified practitioners.

That means a steady stream of new clinics arrive at Pabau on day one, recommended by the people who taught them.

**Recognized name connections.** Pabau has a decent brand footprint in the UK aesthetics space, with relationships across the industry — including SKN clinics (a large chain of clinics) and a number of Harley Street clinicians.

The UK aesthetics world is small at the top end, and being associated with the right names carries weight.

**The "outgrown another tool" moment.** A frequent pattern across markets: customers come to Pabau after they've hit the ceiling with their current software.

They've grown to the point where it can't keep up — too many manual workarounds, too many missing features, too many things being tracked outside the system.

**For your writing:** for UK audiences, Pabau isn't a cold introduction — for many readers, we're already the system their trainers used, or a name they've heard from peers. That's useful context when shaping content for UK aesthetics.

The *"we've outgrown another tool"* trigger is also a relatable angle across all markets, worth leaning into for prospective customers.

One important bit of caution: when you name specific customers in content, always verify with the team first — customer relationships in this space change, and big names in UK aesthetics are sometimes on competing platforms. Getting it wrong is a credibility hit.

## **The product family**

### **Pabau (the core platform)**

The core Pabau platform is where most of the functionality lives. It covers what a clinic needs to run day-to-day:

* **Calendar and scheduling** — appointments across the team  
* **Online booking** — clients booking themselves in  
* **Client records** — full patient files with treatment history  
* **Digital forms and consent** — medical questionnaires, intake, treatment-specific forms  
* **Before-and-after photos** — captured and tied to records  
* **Treatment notes**   
* **Inventory and stock management**  
* **Payments and invoicing**  
* **Marketing and patient communications**  
* **Reporting and analytics**  
* **AI Scribe** 

This isn't the full feature list, but it's the core set you'll find yourself writing about most often.

### **Separate products**

Alongside the core platform, two genuinely separate products sit on top. 

Our hardware products (the card terminals) and the iOS app. 

When you mention either in external content, qualify it with a description — most readers won't know what *Pabau GO* means from the name alone.

| Product | What it is | How to refer to it |
| ----- | ----- | ----- |
| Pabau GO | The mobile app for practitioners (iOS only — no Android version) | *Pabau GO, our iOS app for practitioners* |
| Pabau Pay | Our card terminals for in-person payments | *Pabau Pay, our card terminals* |

Once you've introduced a product in a piece of writing, you can use the name freely after that.

### **A note on naming**

A couple of things you might hear called by a brand name within the team are actually features of the core Pabau platform, not separate products:

* **Online booking** — internally sometimes called *Pabau Connect*. **Don't use that name externally** — customers and prospects won't recognize it. Just say *"online booking"* or *"our online booking portal."*  
* **Pabau Scribe** — the AI scribe feature. It's branded externally as *Pabau Scribe*, so that name is fine to use — just remember it's a feature of the platform, not a standalone product, and qualify it on first mention (*"Pabau Scribe, our AI scribe"*).

### **Care Pathways (formely the patient journey)**

One feature worth knowing about specifically: within **Pabau GO** (our iOS app for practitioners), there's a structured **care pathway** for the period before an appointment.

Patients are guided to complete medical forms, sign consents, and get before-photo prompts ahead of their visit. For the practitioner, this shows up in the app as a checklist of pre-appointment steps.

Everything the patient completes flows into their client record automatically.

It's a strong story in content about cutting admin and improving the client experience — what could be a back-and-forth on email or paper becomes a self-service journey the patient completes when it suits them.

**For your writing:** integrations are a strong angle when comparing Pabau to standalone tools. The headline is *"Pabau connects to the tools you already use — and what it doesn't connect to natively, it can reach through Zapier, Make, or the API."*

This matters because *"do you integrate with X?"* is a common pre-purchase question, and the answer should almost never be a hard no.

## **How customers pay**

Pabau's pricing is a bit different from a typical SaaS tool. Worth understanding so you don't accidentally write something inaccurate.

### **Core subscription**

Pricing is based on **location and number of users.**

A single-location clinic with three practitioners pays less than a multi-location group with 30\. The subscription scales with the business.

**Every customer gets every feature.** We don't have a "starter tier" that locks people out of advanced functionality.

Whether someone is paying for one user or fifty, they get the full Pabau platform.

**For your writing:** this is a real differentiator and worth leaning on. A lot of SaaS tools gate features behind higher tiers — Pabau doesn't.

When you're writing for prospects who might be comparing tools, "no feature gating" is a strong message.

### **Plus add-ons**

A few products sit outside the core subscription as paid monthly add-ons:

* [**Marketing Plus**](https://pabau.com/features/plus/marketing/) — additional marketing capabilities for clinics that want to do more  
* [**Care Plus**](https://pabau.com/features/care-plus/) — additional patient care features  
* **Insights Plus** — additional reporting and analytics (not live yet)

**For your writing:** two things to keep in mind.

First, never undermine the core tools when you describe Plus add-ons. Every subscription comes with full marketing, patient care, and reporting functionality — Plus products are for clinics that want to do more in a specific area, not fixes for gaps in what they already have. Avoid framings like *"basic marketing tools"* or *"limited reporting"* when describing the core.

Second, when you're writing about something that lives inside a Plus product, make it clear it's a Plus add-on rather than something every customer gets out of the box.

### **Hardware**

Pabau Pay card terminals have no monthly fee — customers pay through transaction fees on the payments they take.

We offer two terminal models for different setups.

### **Credits**

Some features run on a credit-based system rather than being included in the subscription:

* SMS messaging  
* Two-factor authentication codes sent by SMS (email is also available, at no credit cost)  
* Telehealth  
* Pabau Scribe usage

Customers top up credits as needed.

## **Why there's no free trial**

Pabau isn't an out-of-the-box tool you can sign up for and start using on a Tuesday afternoon.

The platform is genuinely powerful, and that comes with a setup cost — getting it configured for a specific clinic's workflow takes time and structured support.

A free trial would set customers up to fail.

They'd log in, find the system overwhelming, and assume Pabau isn't for them — when really, the issue is that proper setup is essential to get value out of it.

That's why we offer demos and structured onboarding instead. It's a deliberate choice, not a gap.

**For your writing:** if a piece of content touches on getting started with Pabau, lean into the structured onboarding rather than apologizing for the absence of a trial.

The framing is *"we set you up to succeed,"* not *"we don't have a trial."*

## **The Pabau customer journey**

Customers move through three stages once they sign up. Each stage has a different team supporting them and a different focus.

### **1\. Onboarding — data migration**

When a new customer signs up, the first job is getting their existing data into Pabau — client records, appointment history, inventory, and so on.

This is handled by the onboarding team and is largely a technical exercise: making sure nothing gets lost in the move.

Two things support customers through this stage:

* **The Pabau Portal** — an onboarding portal we built that documents everything customers need to know about getting set up. If a customer is trying to do something or figure something out, the answer's in there.  
* **A dedicated Client Coordinator** — a specific role whose job is making sure each customer is properly supported through onboarding and has everything they need. Customers aren't left to navigate it alone.

**For your writing:** the support around onboarding is a real differentiator and worth leaning on.

New Pabau customers get a documented portal *and* a dedicated person looking out for them — they're never navigating onboarding solo.

### **2\. Customer Success — account optimization**

Once the data is in, customers move to Customer Success for **account optimization.**

This is where the core features get set up properly: booking rules, client communications, forms, payment flows, and the various other moving parts that make Pabau work for a specific clinic.

This stage matters more than people realize.

Pabau is feature-rich, and if customers don't do the groundwork up front, they tend to churn — not because the platform is bad, but because they never actually got it set up to do the things they need it to do.

Account optimization is what makes the difference between a customer who sticks around and one who quietly drifts off.

**For your writing:** account optimization is the make-or-break stage, and worth treating with the weight it deserves in any onboarding or implementation content.

The customers who churn are usually the ones who skipped or rushed this part. Set the expectation that proper setup is what unlocks the value — not a chore on the way to using the software.

### **3\. Revenue team — unlocking more value**

Once a customer is set up and using the core features, they're handed over to the **Revenue team.**

The job here is helping customers get more out of the system by activating features they haven't yet adopted.

This works on three fronts:

* **Stripe for payments.** Being on Stripe activates a lot of additional functionality across the platform, which means customers get more value out of Pabau overall — and tend to stick with us longer as a result.  
* **Pabau Pay card terminals.** The team helps customers activate Pabau Pay so in-person payments run through the same system as the rest of their Pabau setup — no separate payment provider to reconcile.  
* **Plus add-ons.** The Revenue team also helps customers identify when a Plus product (Marketing, Care, or Insights) would unlock capabilities they need but don't currently have.

**For your writing:** the framing here is always customer benefit, not commercial logic. We promote these because they unlock more of the platform for the customer — that's the angle to lead with in any external content.

Lean on what the customer gets (more capabilities, smoother workflows, more of the platform working together) rather than the mechanics of the upsell.
